BMRB’s CS-Rosetta server on the OSG

The OSG is making the derivation of three-dimensional protein structures from NMR chemical shift data, a long-time holy grail of the biological NMR community, routine.

It has long been known that the chemical shift data for a protein contains sufficient information to constrain a protein into at least a set of consistent three-dimensional conformations. Recently, three groups have collaborated to develop algorithms that successfully solve this problem when provided a relatively complete set of chemical shift data.

The result, the CS-Rosetta platform published by the Baker, Bax, and Montelione groups, has been implemented on the OSG. On a single server, a common CS-Rosetta job generating 10-20,000 structures with an 80-100 residue protein would take months to complete, but usually finishes in about two days on the OSG. A CS-Rosetta job with a larger protein (120-130 residues) generating 20,000 structures takes three to four days. Additional NMR conformational restraints data in the form of distance restraints and residual dipolar couplings can be included in a CS-Rosetta run, usually improving the convergence on a high quality low energy structure.

With the OSG CS-Rosetta installation, the Biological Magnetic Resonance Data Bank (BMRB) is conducting CS-Rosetta structure calculation on hundreds of BMRB protein chemical shift data entries. The resulting database can be mined to evaluate the robustness of the CS-Rosetta platform across a wide range of input data characteristics including, data content and completeness, data outliers, protein size, amino acid content, sequence, and secondary structure element content. The information derived from this work will provide insights into the minimum requirements for a successful CS-Rosetta outcome and possible methods for improving the algorithm.

~ Eldon Ulrich

OSG System Administrator Visitor Program

It is my pleasure to announce the new OSG System Administrator Visitor Program, aimed to open a new avenue for collaboration between the Open Science Grid (OSG) and South American institutions.

We are offering the opportunity for two system administrators from South America to stay and work for three to six months at one of two OSG institutions: Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory (Fermilab) in Batavia, Illinois, and the OSG Grid Operation Center (GOC) in Indianapolis, Indiana. This is an excellent opportunity for system administrators to work on large-scale environments and get skills that can be used at their local home institutions. Students and graduates who are working as system administrators at grid sites for their National Grid Infrastructure are invited to apply.

Please help us to spread this announcement by forwarding it to any colleagues who may be interested.

For more information, instructions on how to apply, or if you're interested in becoming a host institution, please visit the program's web page. The deadline for applications is February 29, 2012. You can also contact the program organizers with further questions or comments.

We look forward to working with you!

Introducing the WLCG Technology Evolution Groups

The Worldwide Large Hadron Collider (LHC) Computing Grid (WLCG), which the ALICE, ATLAS, CMS and LHCb VOs participate in, has successfully wrapped up a second year of proton-proton data taking by the LHC. As successful as it has been in supporting LHC computing in the past two years, it must continue to evolve its statement of needs and technology platform.

But who comes up with technical strategies for the WLCG? The Management Board decided to split the work into six "Technology Evolution Groups (TEGs): Data Management, Storage Management, Workload Management, Databases, Security Model, and Operations. The TEGs are meant to review their areas in depth, and come up with a report covering the "state of the art" and strategy document for the next two to five years.

In each technical area, there will be work to understand requirements in light of the last two years of running, lessons learned about the solution's complexity/costs, and the partnerships the WLCG maintains with the middleware. The groups hope to further examine the long-term support of the software stack and understand commonalities between experiments where possible, while respecting the important differences.

Each TEG is composed of representatives from the LHC experiments, site representatives, and infrastructure representatives. OSG has at least one staff member in each TEG, based on their expertise. We are eager to help WLCG design a great strategy for the future. It's a tall order, especially given the relatively short timeline we have - about three months - to finish the work. We're all looking forward to seeing what we turn up!

~ Brian Bockelman

Pilot Project for Transition of Functionality of the DOE Grids CA to OSG

Currently, Open Science Grid depends on the PKI certificate authority (CA) operated by the Department of Energy’s ESnet through its DOE Grids CA. However, OSG has made an agreement with ESnet and DOE Advanced Scientific Computing Research to transition support for this functionality to OSG. Our goal is to have a smooth transition without affecting the quality and availability of service for OSG users.

OSG worked with ESnet to develop and evaluate transition options and present recommendations on the best path forward. The path we recommended is to prototype outsourcing the CA to a commercial company, DigiCert?. This pilot project phase is being actively pursued and an initial agreement with DigiCert? for use of their certificates for the prototyping has been reached. This “OSG CA Activity” is being led by Von Welch of Indiana University with a team including Mine Altunay, Jim Basney and Chander Sehgal.

The pilot project plan includes acquiring both user and host certificates from DigiCert? and testing them in the OSG Trash/Trash/Integration Testbed (ITB) with several different applications from the major OSG communities. The first certificates have already been issued and the first tests were completed successfully. Next, we will be testing with the OSG processes such as the Registration Authority Interface and scripts that obtain host certificates.

The goal is for OSG to implement a replacement for the DOE Grids CA into production in the next 12 months. Moving forward, we will post updates on our progress and plans in each newsletter. We are also working with ESNET to provide ongoing communications to our users. Please visit for the latest news on this project . Further information is also available in the OSG Document Repository.

If you have any questions or concerns, please do not hesitate to contact me, or the security team.

~ Ruth Pordes

Major New Software Release

This December, the OSG Software Team released a major new release: OSG Software 3.0. While there are many differences from the previous version, OSG Software 1.2, the biggest change is that we have moved our distribution mechanism from Pacman to the community standard RPM and yum. This changes many details of how the software is installed, configured, and maintained. As previously announced, we will only be providing critical updates to the older Pacman-based software releases and all new features will be delivered via this new RPM-based release. We believe that this change will better meet the needs of the OSG community.

As of this writing, OSG Software 3.0 supports most of the software we shipped as part of OSG Software 1.2, including the Compute Element, the Storage Element (Bestman, Xrootd, HDF), RSV, the Client, the worker node client, and glexec. We do not yet support GUMS or the Gratia Collector, but those will arrive in early 2012. We encourage you to try out this new RPM-based version of OSG Software because we believe that it is ready to work in production. You can ask support via the usual channels; if you need any help with the transition from Pacman to RPM, please feel free to ask for help.

If you are interested in more details, you can read the documentation and the planning that went into this process.

~ Alain Roy

Featured Site: Midwest Tier 2 Center

Site name:Midwest Tier 2 Center
Location: University of Chicago, Indiana University and University of Illinois (beginning in 2012)
Owner: ATLAS
Job Slots: 4200 and growing
Used by: ATLAS; HCC; GLOW; Engage; DZERO
Opportunistic access: 200 job slots on a continuous basis; opportunistic backfill during dips in ATLAS production

What is unusual about your site?
We are a three-site federation with an average separation distance of 142 miles and more importantly, a network latency of about 4 ms round trip time. We are working on improving our inter-site bandwidth with investments in shared network infrastructure in Chicago (Starlight), as well as peerings to other sites in OSG and WLCG.

What are the greatest challenges your site faces?
There are two: data access and user code behavior. Managing more than six million files from ATLAS and the services that provide inter-site data access is the greatest challenge. Close behind is supporting a broad class of applications submitted by more than 532 unique grid users in the past year.

If you could change one thing about OSG what would it be?
If we could change one thing in OSG it would be to provide a better exchange system or process for opportunistic computing cycles so as to incentivize providing shared resources. Another would be to lower the barrier of entry for new scientific communities.

~ Rob Gardner & Midwest Tier 2 Center

Electron Ion Collider Simulations on OSG

The Electron Icon Collider (EIC) is a proposed future facility that will collide high-energy electrons with light and heavy ions in order to make ultra-high-precision measurements of, among other things, the detailed quantum chromodynamic (QCD) structure of nuclei. In order to guide the design of the interaction region in the EIC's detectors, the physics of the collisions are simulated with various kinds of Monte Carlo event generators. We have developed one such generator called Sartre for a particular measurement; namely, that of the spatial structure of gluons inside ions.

The spatial QCD structure of a nucleus can be determined from a function called the cross-section for a certain kind of electron-ion collision event, in which only one extra particle is produced. Unfortunately, the nature of the calculation makes it extremely time consuming; the cross-section takes about half an hour for each collision on a typical computer. Therefore, the amplitudes of the cross-section for a representative set of collisions are calculated in advance of the actual simulation and stored in look-up tables, which can then be used by Sartre to simulate millions of collisions in a matter of minutes. However, our first estimate on how long all look-up tables of interest would take to produce was approximately 125 CPU-years. Therefore we turned to other resources and came into contact with the Open Science Grid (OSG) User Support team. With their help we have now set up a framework for calculating these look-up tables on the OSG.

The first three months were spent on ensuring numerical stability in the calculations, and to test which granularity we would require in the lookup tables. The testing phase took about 85,000 CPU-hours and the rough estimate for what we will use for production of the first tables is around 700,000 CPU-hours. So far we have constructed the lookup tables for several types of final state particles in electron-gold collisions. The result from the production of the J/Psi meson is shown in the figure, where the coherent (red) curve can be used to obtain the spatial distribution of gluons in the gold nucleus, and the incoherent (blue) curve can access the variance, or lumpiness, in the gluon positions.

~ Tobias Toll, BNL

Trash/Engagement VOMS Server Upgraded

A long time ago, when the construction of the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) had barely started at CERN, the VO Management Registration Service (VOMRS) was developed to provide functionality needed by the experiment that would not otherwise exist. Since then, VOMRS has been used at Fermilab, CERN, Brookhaven National Laboratory, Texas Technology University, as well as in Australia, Germany, and Greece.

In 2008 it was agreed between the software teams and the Worldwide LHC Computing Grid (WLCG) that, in order to simplify operations and support and reduce redundancy, VOMS development would continue until it provided all the needed functionality that VOMRS provides.

Recently that work culminated in the new release of the VOMS-admin service that implements a big chunk of the identified requirements and can replace VOMRS for VOs (not using the third party integration feature such as interfacing to the CERN HR system); it was released as part of the European Grid Infrastructure's (EGI) latest release. In OSG, the Engage VO has been recently transitioned to this VOMS version.

We are hoping that it will be possible to migrate all major VOMRS installations to VOMS and to retire VOMRS when the next version of VOMS is released in spring of 2012.

~ Tanya Levshina
