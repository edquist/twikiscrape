Production Report from CMS

CMS continues to process simulation data in preparation for the long run ahead. We hear that 900 GeV collisions are imminent, and hope we can start taking data at 7 GeV as scheduled on the 30th of March! We also continue to scale up our US CMS Tier 3 program — we have more than a dozen Tier 3 sites on OSG. We expect to continue to scale up with support from our T3 crew, Rob Snihur and Doug Johnson.

~ Burt Holzman

OSG Software Evolution Proposals

As I talked about at the All Hands meeting, one of my goals is to improve communication about upcoming changes to the OSG software stack. So I have started something new that I call “Software Evolution Proposals” (SEPs).

In addition to Software Evolution Proposals which defines what a SEP is, there are a few others, including:
- Dropping support for old platforms, particularly RHEL 3, RHEL4-ia64, and Debian 4.
- Proposed changes in the next two OSG software releases.

I’m interested in your feedback about these proposals. Please feel free to get in touch with me and let me know what you think!

~ Alain Roy
OSG Software Coordinator

  OSG All Hands - iSGTW Feature Summary


Gary Stiehr, left, chats with Anup Mahurkar, the director of software engineering at the Institute for Genome Sciences at the University of Maryland's School of Medicine. The two researchers, who had not previously met, discovered similarities in their work as a result of the presentations they gave during the biology applications workshop.

One hundred eighty-three researchers and vendors gathered for the All Hands Meeting.In addition to workshops for US ATLAS and US CMS computing, sessions covered security, virtualization, cloud computing, biology applications, reports from European colleagues, and the future of US cyberinfrastructure. This year also marked the first vendor and e-demonstration session.

“Several people expressed pleasure at the dynamic discussions that occurred during the panel-style sessions,” said Paul Avery, co-chair of the OSG Consortium Council.

Difficulties with ease of use was a common theme throughout the three-day meeting. Small groups without the resources to hire a full-time cluster manager tend to have difficulty getting set up on OSG, and less computer-savvy users may have trouble navigating steps such as obtaining a security certificate.

Gary Stiehr, left, chats with Anup Mahurkar, the director of software engineering at the Institute for Genome Sciences at the University of Maryland’s School of Medicine. The two researchers, who had not previously met, discovered similarities in their work as a result of the presentations they gave during the biology applications session.

“Grid sites are less prepared in the bioinformatics field,” said Gary Stiehr, the information systems group leader at the Washington University School of Medicine’s Genome Sequencing Center. “Starting conversations surrounding virtualization within Open Science Grid was a good first step for lowering the barrier for a broader set of scientific applications.”

To download slides from presentations given at the meeting, visit the event’s timetable.

—Miriam Boon, iSGTW

OSG Constortium Meeting Welcome from Fermilab

From the OSG White Paper and Roadmap in 2003 to the Consortium today.
From the white paper:

From the OSG White Paper and Roadmap in 2003 to the Consortium today. From the White paper:
“The Open Science Grid will ensure that current and future particle and nuclear physics experiments benefit maximally from the large computing, data storage, network and grid investments already made and planned for. The Open Science Grid will ensure that the U.S. plays a leading role in defining and operating the global grid infrastructure needed for large-scale collaborative and international scientific research. The Open Science Grid will provide a set of services that can be enriched as new science areas choose to join and federate their resources. For the first time combined computing resources at several DOE labs and at dozens of universities will effectively become a single national computing infrastructure for science, the Open Science Grid”.

We have moved from the ideas and ambitious vision to build something of sustained value to scientific communities, to the broad community and successful infrastructure of today.

The All-hands meeting is a great time to reflect on the successes, to strengthen the partnerships, to iron out some problems, and to remember to think about and paint the big picture of where the Consortium is going and what, together, we are trying to achieve.

Fermilab is proud to be a founding member of OSG and a strongly supportive member of this OSG collaborative community .

Full presentation is available on the OSG All Hands Agenda

~Vicky White

OSG Software Ecosystem Workshop

Software representatives from several OSG VOs assembled at a workshop with researchers James Herbsleb and James Howison from Carnegie-Mellon University to discuss their ongoing study of the OSG Virtual Organization scientific software development ecosystems. Attendees from SBGrid, STAR, Engage, IceCube?, LIGO, US-CMS, US-ATLAS, UK e-Science/MyExperiment & Taverna, EGEE, and OSG presented papers answering a series of questions regarding scientific workflows, software architecture, software policies, career paths for software developers, and challenges in the production and sharing of software in our environment.

The results from the February workshop have been documented and are under review by the attendees. The issues and challenges in the Scientific Software Ecosystem were identified and factors leading to these challenges were discussed. The issues were classified in the following areas: 1) software reuse and recreating software from scratch; 2) sustaining high-quality software of over long time horizons; 3) tension between innovation and standards/commonalities; 4) reproducibility of scientific results from the software over time; 5) funding models’ affect on software; and 6) software policies of funders.

This study of the OSG software ecosystem will continue through 2012.

~ Jim Weichel

From the Executive Director

Thank you so much to all who helped, attended, presented at, and contributed to the OSG All Hands Meeting. An event well done! But there is no relaxing. The LHC has restarted data taking; the Tevatron has record luminosity; we are proceeding with reviewing and updating the collaborations documentation; we are working on native packaging of the VDT; we are investigating tools to better handle certificates on the desktop; we are developing a generalized “backup data through the OSG” prototype; we are helping the Tier-3s and smaller sites; and we are planning the summer grid school and blueprint meetings.

It’s quite a lot of work, but it’s a wonderful team that I am sure is up to the challenge!

~ Ruth Pordes
