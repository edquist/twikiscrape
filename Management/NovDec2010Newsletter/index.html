OSG Newsletter 2010: November & December

Structural Biology Grid's Wide Search Molecular

Knowing the atomic resolution three dimensional structure of proteins is critical for understanding protein interactions. Discovering these structures is typically done using X-ray crystallography and a technique known as molecular replacement, which allows the protein crystal scattering pattern to be transformed into a 3D protein geometry by finding fragments of known proteins that effectively produce an equivalent scattering pattern. Unfortunately finding fragments that match can sometimes be very difficult, and in this case it is necessary to use alternative techniques that will typically involve months of additional work.

Enter Wide Search Molecular Replacement, a workflow developed in the lab of Piotr Sliz at Harvard Medical School. The group, part of the SBGrid VO, recently published work on using OSG to help solve the structure of proteins.

Via a publicly accessible web portal, WS-MR allows users to specify a large model search set and parameter search space that can be used to perform years of computing in a few hours and to aggregate the data into a format that allows the user to quickly identify promising results. Although it provides a viable solution only about a quarter of the time, when it does, it typically saves the researcher weeks or months of wet lab or computational work.

Benefiting from opportunistic compute time available on OSG, WS-MR can rapidly initiate thousands of concurrent molecular replacement jobs. The group developed a particular model for the data flow 2 that minimizes data movement, ideally placing input data at each site once and streamlining output to a standard format that reduces the output data returned with each job and allows that data to be continuously integrated into live reports of the scientific results. This is important because a particular set of jobs in a protein study could run for several days. A key part of this system is the use of pilot jobs and glideinWMS, which have improved both reliability and scaling.

Since announcing the availability of the service several weeks ago there has been a steady demand from users and a backlog of jobs.

The group is now focusing on three areas: improving the ability to target searches, including the development of categorized “search sets”; creating a more full-featured web portal that will allow users to log in and better manage data, track jobs and securely share results; and exploring other computationally intensive aspects of structural biology, such as those for electron microscopy protein image processing, that can benefit from grid computing resources.

1, Sliz, Protein structure determination by exhaustive search of Protein Data Bank derived databases, Proc. Nat’l Academy of Sciences doi:10.1073/pnas.1012095107

2 Stokes-Rees, Sliz, Compute and data management strategies for grid deployment of high throughput protein structure studies, IEEE Workshop on Many Task Computing on Grids and Supercomputers 2010 (MTAGS10), Seattle, November 2010.

~ Ian Stokes-Rees

HCC Use Case on OSG

The Holland Computing Center (HCC) successfully completed a “burst” run of 168K wall hours over the weekend of November 6 for a local medical researcher using OSG. At the beginning of November, the researcher came to us with a deadline to finish his work by November 15. He completed ahead of schedule.

Several things came together to make this happen:

– HCC staff assisted the researchers in converting his workflow to run on Condor.

– glideinWMS and Condor powered the underlying submit infrastructure and to stably delivered 6,000 running jobs from a single submit node.

– SRM and GridFTP? were used to deliver input files (about 150MB / job).

– HCC was able to opportunistically use many OSG sites. Previously, HCC clusters provided about half the resources the HCC VO utilized; this time, they were closer to a quarter.

Several required items did not work well, but were eventually overcome: – Our glideinWMS node destabilized above 6,000 jobs or when Condor was used in flocking mode.

– One GridFTP? server crashed during the run, taking many jobs with it. SRM filled its disk partition with log files and crashed. When a GridFTP? or SRM endpoint dies, things start to fail. We will be looking into fixing or mitigating these issues for the “next time,” whenever that might be.

Please view the results.

~ Brian Bockelman

OSG Participation in the CLCAR 2010

The Conferencia Latinoamericana en Computacion de Alto Rendimiento (CLCAR) is an annual Latin American workshop on high performance computing. Although it focuses on HPC, grid and cloud computing are also becoming topics of interest. The latest workshop, held in August in Gramado, Brazil, included a panel discussion about which grid middleware is feasible for the regional national grid infrastructures in development. South American researchers are considering an international grid infrastructure for all of South America as a unique platform or as a federation of medium-size grid projects. Two major platforms were presented during the discussion panel: gLite by Diego Carvalho and OSG by myself. My presentation familiarized the Latin American community with OSG, and several countries have joined the OSG outreach effort in the region.

~ Jose Caballero

South American Grid School

The most recent OSG grid school was held the first week of December in Sao Paulo, Brazil. Around 40 students attended from multiple science disciplines and 23 institutions. The school, focused on end-users’ needs, taught students step by step how to join the Grid community, adapt their scientific applications to the new technology and use it efficiently. Also, students learned how to run large-scale computing applications, first locally and then using the OSG.
Two open discussions took place, along with one Q&A session with Ruth Pordes and Miron Livny and hands-on exercises. Other OSG staff who lectured included Dan Fraser, Tanya Levshina, Zack Miller, Igor Sfiligoi. Joel Snow lectured on the experience of the D0 high-energy physics experiment using OSG resources, enabling attendees to see how all the concepts and techniques they learned during the week have been applied by a real experiment. A working meeting between the GOC’s Rob Quick and those developing and deploying a local grid operation center allowed the sharing of ideas and experiences. Two special lectures by invited local speakers, one on high performance computing and one on data storage, completed the school.

Some comments from students:

“The OSG School in Sao Paulo was very good. The Grid concepts were presented in a theoretical and practical way, with a focus on the main goal of any Grid infrastructure: ‘collaboration.’
- Charles Rodamilans, PhD? student of Computer Engineering

“… the most impressive course that I have attended, not because of the size of the Open Science Grid, but because of the ability and motivation that instructors demonstrated throughout the course. They were informative and technically knowledgeable. “
- Eriksen Costa, Software architect

“The school was fantastic! … It gave me a broadened view of what a grid is, what it is useful for and how I can use it.”
- Griselda Garrido, neuroscience researcher

~ Jose Caballero

 

Production Report for CMS

CMS has wrapped up the 2010 proton-proton run, taking advantage of the earnest efforts of the LHC to continually increase luminosity. We recorded more than 43 pb^-1 during the run and are working hard on analysis. The fun isn’t over yet, though — the machine has now started on the heavy ion program, and has ramped from zero collisions to 120×120 bunches in a week! And on November 18th, we announced the detection of Z bosons from the heavy ion program — This the first time Z bosons have been observed in heavy ion collisions. We look forward to the coming weeks as we accumulate more data!

~ Burt Holzman

Improving Documentation

Composing great technical documentation--documentation that reassures the reader that they will achieve their goals efficiently by following the instructions presented--requires patience to review and test text that will then be gradually improved in an iterative process.

The Content Management Project was founded in September 2009 with the goal to improve the documentation released with OSG software and tools . Since then, 419 documents have cleaned up and reduced to 277 documents that have been integrated into a peer-review-like process. Since April, a documentation team from the OSG community has led the review, improvement and release of 164 of these documents, 59% of all documents involved in the process.

The Content Management Project is a community project of the Open Science Grid. As such its success entirely depends on volunteers picking up the many small documentation tasks. If you’ve ever wished for better OSG documentation, please help us to make it happen! We are constantly looking for people who would like take ownership of a document and who will, in turn, be supported and guided by members of the documentation team while improving the document osg-docs@listserv.fnal.gov.

~ Robert Engel

VO Forum Update

We are striving to bring in more technical experts to present tools and information that may be of use to VOs. On Dec. 2, Tech-X Corporation representatives presented an introduction on SVOPME, a collaborative project between Tech-X and Fermilab to provide a scalable privileges management environment. SVOPME consists of tools for VOs to define and publish desired resource access privileges and for sites to assist in configuring appropriate access policies. The project is looking for VOs who are interested in using this technology.

In addition, OSG Storage team member Doug Strain, developer of Pigeon Tools recently presented at this forum. Pigeon Tools is a method of easily creating extendable classes of tests to evaluate the viability of SEs for VOs.

The CDF Offline team is working out issues on running their data processing jobs at the Korea Institute of Science and Technology Information (KISTI), which provides dedicated resources to CDF.

The Large Synoptic Survey Telescope (LSST) recently finished Phase 2 of their run on OSG, simulating an entire night of telescope observation (1000 images with the 3200 MegaPixel? camera). The LSST simulation group successfully performed image validation and are now planning the next phase.

Gearing up for their December 17 software release, GEANT-4 is conducting validation testing using opportunistic computing on OSG at half a dozen sites.

The Holland Computing Center (HCC) successfully completed a priority workflow in early November. 110,000 CPU hours, 26 TB were transferred. Read more about this accomplishment in “HCC Usage on OSG” in this newsletter.

~ Marcia Teckenbrock
