OSG takes lead in Einstein@Home

By taking advantage of available opportunistic cycles on the Open Science Grid, LIGO is contributing an average of 140,000 CPU hours every day to the search for gravitational waves; this application produces 1 out of every 10 current results for the Einstein@Home Project. This result has been achieved by recent improvements to the methods used by the E@H application to run on the OSG; by enabling support for Condor-G, we allow our application to scale up to the many cores available on large OSG sites. And by using automated error detection and recovery methods, we were also able to reduce the staff effort required to operate this production to about one staff hour for every 100,000 cpu hours worth of scientific results; this has been key in scaling up to the 32 grid resources that are currently used by LIGO E@H.

~ Robert Engel

Production Report from CMS

In CMS, we’re currently analyzing the collision data from the 2009 run.

We are happy to announce that we had our first paper in the LHC energy regime accepted for publication in the Journal of High Energy Physics. It presents the measurement of multiplicity distributions as a function of pseudo-rapidity and transverse momentum at center of mass energies of 900 and 2360 GeV.

We’re continuing to analyze—but also we are preparing ourselves for the very long 2010-2011 run that lies ahead!

~ Burt Holzman

Earthquake Workflow

The Southern California Earthquake Center is developing physics-based seismic hazard maps of Southern California. The maps require the execution of workflows composed of a few MPI codes and of almost a million single-processor tasks. The MPI tasks are best suited for the high-end capabilities of the TeraGrid?, while the single-processor tasks can take advantage of the opportunistic resources provided by OSG. In a recent effort that brought together scientists from SCEC, members of the OSG storage team, the University of Nebraska-Lincoln site administrators, and the Pegasus team, the SCEC CyberShake? workflow was able to run automatically across the TeraGrid? and OSG with the MPI jobs being scheduled by Pegasus on Ranger and the sequential jobs being sent to Firefly. Data transfer between the sites and final data transfers were performed automatically by Pegasus as part of the overall workflow. The workflow took approximately three days to complete without resource provisioning. See CyberShake.

~ Ewa Deelman, USC

Campus Grid Working Meeting

As all OSG site managers realize, there are special challenges and opportunities connecting new campus resources and researchers to the OSG infrastructure: What are the incentives for new faculty, IT managers, and even CIOs to connect precious local community resources to a national infrastructure such as the OSG? How do campus-level and OSG-level sharing models compare? What best practices exist for provisioning and managing resources across the campus? What technologies are best suited to create seamless user environments for both job and data management for local and Grid-based computation?

These were some of the questions considered during an OSG working meeting at Fermilab last January. Participants from Clemson University, Fermilab, Purdue University, RENCI, SUNY-Buffalo, UCLA, University of Chicago, University of Nebraska, and the University of Wisconsin contributed to a set of lively discussions. An important outcome of the meeting was recognition that OSG is a good forum for sharing best practices and knowledge about building and evolving such infrastructure within the campus. Conversely, given the importance of sharing and connecting campus resources to a national computing infrastructure, it was recognized that OSG should develop a comprehensive strategy across all existing work areas to support these connections.

A summary report from the workshop is available in the OSG Campus Grids wiki.

~ Rob Gardner

The OSG Campfire Turns One

What happens when several people are troubleshooting a technical issue online? Most often it turns into a lot of back-and-forth—essentially a high latency question-and-answer session resulting in long email threads.

Since many issues can be more efficiently resolved with conversational real-time instant-messaging, the OSG Sites and Trash/Trash/Integration teams introduced an OSG-dedicated persistent chat room service last year to assist with troubleshooting. Two web-based chat rooms—one for general issues and one for education and training workshops—were created using a service called Campfire.

Campfire has a number of features, including compact and attractive rendering of screen snippets (useful when debugging console output), file and image management, and transcript logging. Conversational logs and snippets are stored indefinitely and are available via search later to revisit conversations and issue resolution.

While the chat room is open to anyone in OSG at any time, technical personnel are on hand at least three days a week for three hours at a time to help site administrators with service issues (installation, troubleshooting, optimization, etc.). Using Campfire requires only a web browser, however, some members opt to use third-party add-ons and clients supporting features like desktop drop-n-drag , use with iPhones, and twitter feeds. More information about Campfire.

~ Rob Gardner

Teaching a Grid Course

About every other semester, the experts at the Holland Computing Center embark on one adventure harder than running any grid service: teaching students. During the Fall 2009 semester, we offered Cluster and Grid Computing, a course in the Computer Science and Engineering department at University of Nebraska-Lincoln.

The first half of the course taught the fundamentals of building a cluster from scratch, starting with manual Linux installs and moving to a fully automated deployment. The class was split into eight groups, each maintaining their own four-node cluster. The second half of the course focused on a variety of applications topics—from traditional, like MPI, to hot trends, such as MapReduce?. The course culminated in using Condor (with flocking) as a tool for uniting the classroom clusters into a grid. Of the interesting class projects, some students even ran their science applications on the OSG.

The next time you look at the users running at your cluster, don’t be surprised if you find hard working Nebraska students that have become happy OSG users!

~ Brian Bockelman

From the Executive Director

Meetings in the last month have covered a wide range of topics looking both at  current activities and toward the future. We are looking  for contributors to help with user documentation on the Twiki and also for input on broader campus-wide facilities at your organization.

OSG accounting shows a new high in cpu usage thanks largely to the ramp up of LIGO’s Einstein@Home but also to sustained usage across the board. We have a nice acknowledgement of contributions to the LHC in a Symmetry Magaine article.

We are currently focusing  on details of our operations activities and responses. Our commitment to distributed operations and a federated model means that we use interfaces between many diverse support  organizations—a model which parallels how the global network fabric is supported. We are interested in your help and ideas to ensure timely and complete attention to reported problems.

I look forward to seeing many of you at the All Hands Meeting in March and appreciate all the help we are getting from the program committee. We will be posting the details about each of the sessions over the next week, and we are still looking for a few more submissions for demonstrations!

~ Ruth Pordes
