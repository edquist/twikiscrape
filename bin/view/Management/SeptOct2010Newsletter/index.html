OSG Newsletter, September/October 2010

Running computations of the Large Synoptic Survey Telescope on OSG

The Large Synoptic Survey Telescope (LSST), to be installed at Cerro Pachon, Chile when funded and built, will enable large areas of the sky to be seen and captured with a rapid fire 3.2 Gigapixel camera. LSST can take more than 800 panoramic sky images each night on objects that change or move, such as exploding supernovae and potentially hazardous near-Earth asteroids. Covering the sky twice each week, it will produce data at a rate comparable to the LHC experiments.

In its first month of operation LSST will see more of the Universe than all previous telescopes combined, enabling an unprecedented 3-D map of the Cosmos, providing multiple probes of dark matter and dark energy. Over the course of its ten years of operation, a data set of several hundred petabytes will be accumulated.

Purdue LSST collaborator Ian Shipsey wanted to understand how OSG could be used for LSST computing. OSG Trash/Engagement agreed to port its image simulation application to the OSG, and in June the OSG Executive Board appointed a task force to explore whether OSG could run the application as a production activity.

The application workflow runs in two steps. First, one Grid job splits a 500 MB catalog file into 189 pieces, one per chip of the CCD camera. Second, two sets of 189 independent Grid jobs produce two images with different exposures from the same input. The resulting output consists of 25 MB per chip, about 5 GB per image. Each chip-simulation job lasts less than 4 hours, and simulating 1000 images consumes about 800,000 CPU hours. To manage this workload, we have chosen the Glidein WMS system with a submission node provided by the University of Nebraska-Lincoln.

Production started in August using on average 50,000 CPU hours per day of opportunistic resources (i.e. 2000 cores continuously at 13 sites). The project is now 95% complete, with the validation process still under way with encouraging results.

We are currently working with the image simulation group to understand how to use OSG as an official image production facility. Also, the LSST data management group is interested in integrating some of their data-intensive pipelines with OSG, starting with the moving object detection application.

~ Gabriele Garzoglio

News from the VO Forum

The OSG At-Large Virtual Organizations FY 2010-11 Plans, Needs and Requirements document, which contains input from twenty stakeholders, is available to view.


The OSG security team is beginning a pilot project working on CILogon. The project provides a service that bridges the identity credentials generated by the nation’s universities, through the InCommon? Federation, to a certificate for authentication to NSF’s cyberinfrastructure projects. Details are available at CILogon Project, or contact osg-security for discussion.

OSG is now cooperating with Galois, Inc. in an SBIR project to enhance cross-organizational collaboration in the area of identity, access and authorization.

Marco Mambelli shared a presentation comparing the conference systems Adobe Connect Pro, ReadyTalk? and EVO as well as gave a brief Adobe Connect demo.

A presentation was given at a recent Site Coordination Meeting by Harold Enrique Castro Barrera of the Universidad de los Andes about what he and his colleagues have done in Colombia, deploying their own grid using the OSG infrastructure. VOs are encouraged to read the minutes and view the streaming video via Adobe Connect or Flash video.

Finally, here's a useful tool for viewing GOC tickets.

~ Marcia Teckenbrock

Production Report on CMS

The LHC continues to increase luminosity in leaps and bounds—we recorded more than 3 inverse pb this week, with more to come as the machine continues to increase the number of bunches until we hit the instantaneous luminosity target of 1E32/cm^2 s. We have kept our grid resources busy with over 25k jobs in parallel doing Monte Carlo production and data re-reconstruction. And last but not least, we published a new and interesting result on two-particle correlations which has been accepted by JHEP.

~ Burt Holzman

 

 

 

OSG Storage Forum

This year’s OSG Storage Forum took place at the University of Chicago on September 21 and 22. More than forty people attended the conference in person, while many others connected remotely and listened to particular talks or gave presentations. The forum covered multiple topics, including new trends in storage software adopted by OSG and new leading edge projects such as ExTENCI Wide Area Network File System and Advanced Networking Initiative. The attendees also heard reports from Tier-2 and Tier-3 sites of CMS and ATLAS. They listened to representatives from LIGO, Glue-X and LSST VO about the successes and issues encountered while using OSG storage resources. The panel discussion led by Rob Gardner covered a wide range of topics, including challenges in implementing the global namespace and redirection, p2p file replications, usability of parallel NFS v4.1 and others.


There are lots of useful talks posted on the Storage Forum site.

~ Tanya Levshina

Production Report on ATLAS

LHC beam commissioning of high intensity beams at 7 TeV? is making good progress, with the ATLAS experiment experiencing an exponential growth of integrated luminosity. Through September, ATLAS has recorded about 700M events that were validated for physics. Given substantial software developments since the last reprocessing campaign and in preparation for important conferences and for publication purposes, ATLAS plans to reprocess all validated 7 TeV? events and 500M simulated events starting in late October. This campaign is scheduled to end in mid-November.

The Geant4 simulation campaign began in mid-September and involves all production resources at the Tier-1 and the Tier-2 centers in the U.S. (currently about 10,000 job slots). Also, in view of the upcoming Heavy Ion run at the LHC in November, the worldwide distributed facility is in the process of getting prepared for the specific needs of the nuclear physics community within ATLAS. In September, 1.4 M production and 2.1 M analysis jobs were successfully completed on OSG resources used by ATLAS.

~ Michael Ernst

GlideinWMS Factory Operations Support

Several VOs have recently switched to glideinWMS to run their jobs on OSG, thus taking advantage of the pilot paradigm that makes Grid resources look more uniform.

A major enabler for this switch was the glidein factory operated by the UCSD team on behalf of OSG.

A glidein factory is the link between the VO and the Grid, and thus the most operations intensive element of glideinWMS. By removing that operational load from the VOs, it becomes much easier to effectively use the Grid.

The UCSD team has operated the glidein factory for over a year now, supporting initially CMS only, and then gradually adding other VOs. Until now, the operations was run mostly by graduate students with only a very limited contribution by OSG staff, but this is going to change in the near future with a substantial increase in OSG-provided effort and a formal SLA.

~ Igor Sfiligoi
