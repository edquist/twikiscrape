Update on Production Runs of ROSETTA on OSG from Kuhlman Lab

In the Kuhlman Lab at the University of North Carolina, we have developed a protocol that uses PDB files that contain structures of proteins bound to certain peptide substances in order to scan for the substrates that will bind to a target epitope—the part of the molecule the immune system can attach to. Specifically, we are investigating the protein structures that cause infectious diseases, such as tuberculosis, malaria, and influenza.

We are using ROSETTA, a protein design program, to attach additional residues to the peptide in the PDB file and to run design simulations to test the peptide’s binding affinity. From these results, the best structure is selected based on the calculated binding energy and total energy output by ROSETTA. We will then confirm our results in the laboratory. Currently, two million starting decoys are being designed with ROSETTA on the OSG. Each decoy submits 50,000 jobs on the OSG per week, and currently, I have completed about one quarter of the decoys.

~ Eun-Jung Choi

GHC Presentation

The mission of the Anita Borg Institute for Women and Technology is to seek to increase the impact of women on all aspects of technology and to increase the positive impact of technology on the world’s women. And this is exactly what it achieved again this fall when the institute hosted their annual Grace Hopper Celebration (GHC) Of Women in Computing.

I attended the conference representing both LIGO and the OSG and presented “On the Evolution of Large Scale Distributed Systems AND High Throughput Computing on the Open Science Grid Infrastructure.” I shared the technical session with Corina Stratan from Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam; Corina introduced the audience to the main models of distributed systems and I gave a more detailed view of the structure and function of the OSG and LIGO’s role within it. Our presentations were very well received and met with several questions.

The world’s largest gathering of women in computing, GHC increased by over 11 percent in size this year, with a record 1608 attendees. The conference theme, “Creating Technology for Social Good,” drew participants from industry, academia, and government. More than 173 companies and 210 universities were represented, with about half of attendees being undergraduate and graduate students.

Over several parallel sessions top researchers presented their work, over one hundred panels discussed wide-ranging topics, and numerous special sessions and workshops focused on the role of women in today’s technology fields.

The conference was hustling and bustling with activity from early morning until evening. One could feel intensity and creativity in the air, as every corner was occupied by groups of people talking, networking, and laughing.

Indeed, praise to these talented women who put together a very well organized and content rich gathering-a success all around. I left feeling energized and inspired.

~ Britta Daudert

Grid Colombia Workshop

October’s two-week Grid Columbia Workshop was an expanded version of previous OSG grid schools. We partnered with the PIs of the Grid Colombia initiative to meet their particular objectives as part of a 3-phase program launching of a Colombian national grid initiative. Jose Caballero (OSG’s Latin America outreach coordinator), Rob Gardner, and Grid Colombia’s Harold Castro Barrera (Universidad de los Andes) and Enrique Gonzalez Guerrero (Javeriana Universidad) led the workshop. Thirty-four participants took part—university faculty, technical staff, and students from seventeen universities throughout Colombia.

During the first week, we focused on “Scientific Computing on Grids,” including fundamentals such as job submission, guest lectures and discussion, and lab exercises illustrating use with production systems (Panda, GlideinWMS?, OSG-MatchMaker and Swift) and new tools, such as the OSG storage discovery toolkit. Thanks to volunteers Burt Holzman, Mike Wilde, John McGee? and Mats Rynge, Brian Bockelman, Ted Hessleroth, Pedro Salgado, and Marco Mambelli.

The second week involved “Grid Infrastructure Building” with the goal of building a small test grid consisting of prototype Grid Colombia sites supported by a “site” with central (GOC-like) services. Students were divided into teams who built small clusters from scratch with an additional “GOC team” that would install central services. Guest lectures were given on GUMS (Steve Timm), grid operations (Rob Quick), security best practices (Anand Padmanabhan), and CE installation demonstration (Suchandra Thapa).

Sites reported on their progress each day with problems encountered and next steps. In the end, one site had functional Condor + GUMS + CE + Ganglia systems; three sites had working Condor systems plus additional services, including working GUMS installations; one installed an SE, and several had CE installs in various states of configuration progress. The “GOC team” deployed a ReSS? instance.

We devoted the final day to project planning and developing a program of work for Grid Colombia based on progress made at the workshop. There are enthusiastic plans to deploy twelve Grid Colombia sites by the end of the year using OSG software. Another workshop will occur in February/March, at which OSG has been invited to provide a follow-up component to the site-building modules given here and to offer guidance and assistance in setting up their nascent grid.

Thank you to everyone who helped with the workshop—in particular, the guest experts who stepped up with both intellectual content and personal effort.

From the Executive Director

I much appreciate the focused attention and effort from the many people involved in the recent and ongoing Tier-3 focus work, and I look forward to the payback as new sites come on board. During the past month the US ATLAS Tier-3 coordinators, OSG Tier-3 liaison and site coordinators, and the Storage and Condor groups have had two successful visits to the University of Wisconsin for “hands-on standing up” of a typical Tier-3 site. This is being documented at the Tier 3 Web and will become a pattern to which we can add common instructions, best practices, and other information for Tier-3 administrators—those who generally have only small amounts of time to devote to this activity and who were not necessarily “trained for this job.”

This month’s joint Identity Management Workshop between the ESNET DOEGrids and OSG groups (Security, VOs and Blueprint) was energetic and useful. Thank you to all who organized and attended this meeting. I look forward to the report and recommendations for focus areas and decisions to be made, both short and long term. An immediate response has been to couple the words “Usability” and “Security” together when we talk–—with “Usability” coming first. The goal is for this to concretely impact our actions as we move forward; several small communities are now active for which this is currently a significant hurdle to overcome.

The Open Science Grid magnetic clips are proving popular at SC09— “The OSG clips your users and resources together.” —where there has been interest in OSG from potential users, our partners in Europe, and our agency visitors.

~Ruth Pordes
